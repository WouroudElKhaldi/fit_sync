1. Chest (Pectorals)
These muscles are responsible for bringing the arms forward across the body (horizontal adduction) and pushing movements.

Pectoralis Major (Sternocostal Head): The main mid-to-lower portion of the chest.

Pectoralis Major (Clavicular Head): The upper chest region near the collarbone.

Pectoralis Minor: A smaller muscle deep beneath the pectoralis major that stabilizes the shoulder blade.

Serratus Anterior: The "boxer's muscle" along the side of the ribs, visible on lean athletes when arms are raised.

2. Back
The back consists of several massive overlapping superficial muscle structures responsible for pulling patterns and posture.

Latissimus Dorsi (Lats): The largest wing-like muscles of the upper/mid back responsible for back width and vertical pulling.

Trapezius (Upper, Middle, Lower Traps): The large kite-shaped muscle running from the neck down to the mid-back.

Rhomboids (Major & Minor): Deep muscles situated between the shoulder blades responsible for pulling them together.

Teres Major: Located just above the lats, often referred to as the "little helper of the lat" because it assists in pulling movements.

Erector Spinae: The thick bands of lower back muscle running along the spine responsible for trunk extension and stability during deadlifts.

Infraspinatus & Teres Minor: Part of the rotator cuff group on the back of the shoulder blade, highly trained for shoulder stability.

3. Shoulders (Deltoids)
The shoulder muscle is divided into three distinct heads that control arm movement in different spatial planes.

Anterior Deltoid: The front part of the shoulder, highly active in pressing movements.

Lateral Deltoid (or Medial Deltoid): The side of the shoulder, responsible for bringing the arms outward and creating upper-body width.

Posterior Deltoid: The rear portion of the shoulder, responsible for horizontal arm extensions and pulling motions.

4. Arms
Arm tracking is typically divided between elbow flexion (pulling) and elbow extension (pushing) muscle paths.

Biceps & Forearms (Pull Group)
Biceps Brachii (Long Head): The outer part of the bicep that forms the famous peak.

Biceps Brachii (Short Head): The inner part of the bicep that adds width to the front of the arm.

Brachialis: A powerful muscle sitting deep beneath the lower bicep, highly targeted with hammer grips.

Brachioradialis: The prominent muscle on top of the forearm near the elbow.

Forearm Flexors & Extensors: The group of small muscles responsible for grip strength and wrist control.

Triceps (Push Group)
Triceps Brachii (Long Head): The large inner portion of the tricep running along the back of the arm.

Triceps Brachii (Lateral Head): The outer tricep muscle that creates the classic "horseshoe" shape from the side profile.

Triceps Brachii (Medial Head): The deep mid-to-lower portion of the tricep closer to the elbow.

5. Legs (Lower Body)
The legs comprise the most massive muscle structures in the human anatomy, broken down into major functional regions.

Quadriceps (Front of Thigh)
Rectus Femoris: The central quad muscle that runs down the middle of the thigh.

Vastus Lateralis: The outer quad muscle that forms the "outer sweep" look.

Vastus Medialis: The inner quad muscle just above the knee, known as the "teardrop."

Vastus Intermedius: Sits deep beneath the rectus femoris.

Hamstrings & Posterior Chain (Back of Thigh)
Biceps Femoris (Long & Short Head): The lateral hamstring muscle on the outer back part of the thigh.

Semitendinosus: The middle hamstring muscle.

Semimembranosus: The inner hamstring muscle.

Glutes & Hips
Gluteus Maximus: The main, largest muscle of the buttocks responsible for hip extension power.

Gluteus Medius: Located on the upper outer side of the pelvis, vital for hip stabilization.

Gluteus Minimus: The smallest, deepest glute muscle assisting the medius.

Hip Adductors: The inner thigh muscle group (including Adductor Magnus, Longus, and Brevis) responsible for drawing the legs together.

Calves
Gastrocnemius: The diamond-shaped, visible upper calf muscle (highly active when knees are straight).

Soleus: The wider, flatter calf muscle sitting lower down and deep beneath the gastrocnemius (highly active when knees are bent).

6. Core
The trunk stabilization musculature.

Rectus Abdominis: The classic "six-pack" muscle tracking down the center of the abdomen.

External Obliques: The visible muscles running diagonally down the sides of the waist.

Internal Obliques: Located beneath the external obliques, facilitating torso rotation.

Transverse Abdominis (TVA): The deep inner "corset" muscle responsible for abdominal bracing and intra-abdominal pressure.
